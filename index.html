      ❓ FAQ — connectors & wiring

      NL4d vs NL4s vs NL2 — what's the difference, and why it matters

      A Speakon NL4 is a 4-pole connector with two independent circuits: pins 1+/1− and pins 2+/2−. Amplifier outputs are wired so the channels are spread across the output connectors in a fixed pattern:

      • Physical output 1 is wired NL4d: amp channel 1 on 1+/1− and amp channel 2 on 2+/2− — one connector, two channels.
      • Output 2 is wired NL4s: only amp channel 2, on 1+/1−.
      • Same pattern for 3 & 4: output 3 is NL4d (ch3 on 1+/1−, ch4 on 2+/2−), output 4 is NL4s (ch4 on 1+/1−).
      • So the odd outputs (1 & 3) are NL4d and the even outputs (2 & 4) are NL4s. You'll see exactly this layout on amp / rack patch plates and on NL8 break-out blocks.

      NL4d (dual) carries 2 amp channels on one connector — handy for cross-link daisy: the first cab listens on 1+/1−, the LINK passes 2+/2− through to the next cab, so one NL4d cable feeds two individually-addressable cabs. NL4s (single) is the same 4-pole NL4 connector carrying just one channel (on 1+/1−).

      Why an NL4s is not just an NL2: an NL2 is only a 2-pole connector (1+/1−). An NL4s carries a single channel too, so it's tempting to treat the two as interchangeable — but the panel sockets and break-out blocks are 4-pole NL4, so you cable them with NL4, and the very same socket position can instead be an NL4d carrying a second channel on 2+/2−. Knowing whether an output is NL4d or NL4s tells you how many channels that cable/socket actually carries — which is why this tool tracks them as distinct.

      Cross-link vs parallel loop-through

      A loop-through is a short jumper from one cab's LINK out to the next cab's INPUT. Two flavours:

      • Cross-link (SXH-style) — each cab uses 1 amp channel; the LINK swaps the pin pairs so the next cab receives the other channel. One NL4d feeds 2 cabs, each individually addressable. Used by SXH218/SXHF218, SX218 "Updated", SX218+.
      • Parallel loop-through (stock) — both cabs hang in parallel on the same channel(s). 2 cabs share one channel → the impedance halves (e.g. 2Ω). Used by SX218/SX215/ SX118+ "Default"/stock wiring.

      Bridge mode & 2Ω / 4Ω

      Bridge ties an amp module's two channels together into one higher-power output that drives a single cab (so 2 cabs per iK42). It only makes sense on cross-link wiring — a parallel loop-through cab already uses both pin pairs for its two drivers, so there's nothing to bridge. That's why the Bridge toggle is greyed out on Default/parallel wiring.

      2Ω vs 4Ω (SX215 stock): 2Ω = two cabs in parallel on one channel — fewest amp channels, but a 2Ω load (iK42 only). 4Ω = one cab per channel (no parallel) — doubles the amp channels but an easier 4Ω load.

      Multipin cables & break-out blocks

      Multipin trunks carry several channels in one cable: NL8 = 4 ch, NL16 = 8 ch, Socapex = 8 ch, LK25 = 12 ch. At the array they fan out to individual NL4 cables, in one of two ways:

      • Integrated fan-out cable — one moulded splitter (multipin in, N× NL4 tails out).
      • Passive break-out block — a box with a multipin input and N loose NL4 sockets; you patch your own NL4 cables. This is the default here (Dutch dry-hire convention).

      A 2-stage chain is possible too: LK25 → 3× NL8 → NL4 (each NL8 either an integrated splitter or its own NL8-stage break-out block). And rack-side fan-in combines several loose NL4 jacks into one multipin run when the rack only has NL4 panels.

      Mirror, adjacent & castellated pairing

      • Mirror pairing — stack k pairs with stack N−k+1 (symmetric L/R), so mirrored positions get the same signal; an odd middle stack is a singleton.
      • Adjacent pairing — used in portrait: neighbouring stacks pair up instead of mirroring across the array.
      • Castellated — alternating cardioid (F+B) and single (F) stacks for a half-cardioid array. Presets: uniform, bookend, buttress.
